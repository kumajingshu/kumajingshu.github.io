Making core courses more engaging for underclassmen

Mobile App | Product Design | Interaction Design | UX Research

How can we help teenagers with chronic illness like Joy manage their complex medication needs and improve medication adherence in an unobtrusive way?

To solve this problem, we designed a mobile app integrated with wearable devices to help teenagers with chronic illness manage their medication and remind them to stay on medication routine.

My Role

I worked in a team of 5 as Product Designer and UX Researcher for a client, an Endocrinologist and Professor at the University of Michigan Medical School. In the design phase, I designed the scheduling process and making design recommendations based on the user feedback.

Approach

We adopted the user-centered design and tried to get users and stakeholders involved in the whole process.

User Research

Method

To understand the daily life and challenges of teens with chronic illness, we did:

  • 2 interviews with teenagers with chronic illness, 6 interviews with stakeholders including experts and patients' parents, 2 patient focus groups, and 1 caregiver focus group
  • A questionnaire survey with 41 respondents from target group
Findings
  • Medication adherence is an important part of daily life among teens with chronic illness.
    • The current apps cannot meet their needs of managing complex medication schedule.
    • They tend to forget to take their medicine on non-routine days.
    • Parents are involved in the teenâ€™s medication adherence actively.
    • They use some artifacts, such as pillbox or lunch box, to remind them to take medicine.
  • They don't want people around them to be overly aware of their chronic conditions.
  • They need to track their symptoms to monitor the effect of the medication and report to the doctor.
  • Mobile App is used commmonly by the target users whose age is 12-18.
Persona

Based on the findings above, we built personas to help us understand the users' life and their needs.

Scope

Based on the findings above, we defined the problem we want to solve as:

How to help teenagers with chronic illness stay on their medication routine in an unobtrusive way and manage their complex medication needs easily?

Ideation

Exploration

We brainstormed different ideas and analyzed the pros and cons based on the feedback from users and their caregivers, the client, and some experts.

Digital Sticky Notes Adhesive Patch Smart Water Bottle
Pros The sticky notes can be attached to different artifacts in life and reminds the users. The patch can send alert without attracting the notice of others. Many users also need to drink a lot of water and track their water intake. They can be reminded every time when they drink water. Some teenagers think this water bottle is very cool.
Cons The reminder is too obvious and has many pieces to manage. It is not very practical. The technology has not been adopted widely. Boys might not want to carry a water bottle. The reminder might be obvious.

Decision

After the first round of ideation, we focused on coming up with more practical ideas and decided to design a mobile app integrated with wearable devices. We decided the following key features to make this app more user-friendly than existing products and fit the personality and needs of teenagers.

Design Solution

Goal 1: Improve the scheduling process to meet users' complex scheduling needs
Problem
  • Users may have several medicines on the same schedule or input different doses for different days.
  • Most of the current apps only allow users to add one medicine that has a fixed schedule one time.
  • They hope the naming of the pills can be easy for them to remember.
Solution
  • Allowing users to add multiple schedules in one medicine.
  • Taking a picture of the medicine and giving the medicine a nickname reduces their cognitive burden.
  • "Add to existing schedule" feature allows users to easily add a new medicine to already scheduled events.
  • Adding a medicine directly from a calendar makes scheduling more intuitive.
Goal 2: Get users' loved ones involved in their medication adherence in a more context-aware way
Problem
  • Users highly rely on their caregivers in their medication adherence.
  • It is more difficult to keep their medication on track in some context, like weekends or holidays.
Solution
  • Users can invite their caregivers and close friends to remind them.
  • They can set when they want to be reminded, such as when a medication is missed or on weekends.
Goal 3: Help users track their symptoms in a way that fits teenagers' personality
Problem
  • Users need to monitor the effect of a medicine and talk to their doctors about their symptoms.
  • Current apps don't have features to encourage teen's use.
Solution
  • Users can use customized emoji to record their feeling after taking a medicine.
  • They can view their adherence and the effects of a medication over time.
Goal 4: Remind the users in an unobtrusive way
Problem
  • Users don't want people around them to be overly aware of their illness.
  • Wearable device can be a solution, but most of the current apps are not integrated with wearables.
Solution
  • Users can connect this app with a wearable device or other smart artifacts.
  • Customizable vibration pattern helps them be reminded to take a medicine without catching others' attention.

Iteration

We tested the prototype with 2 users in person and 6 users online, and then iterated on the design based on the user feedback. The biggest change we made was the task of "adding a medicine to a new schedule".

Design (Round 1)
User feedback
  • They want both a nickname of pills which helps them remember the pills, and the real name which can be auto-filled and makes sense for the parents and doctors.
  • The dosage and schedule should be at the same place rather than in separate steps.
  • They may have multiple schedules for one medicine.
  • They hope to the app allows them to add a medicine via calendar as well.
Design (Round 2)
  • Simplified the process by combining the dosage and schedule to one page.
  • Allowed users to add both nickname and official name for a medicine.
  • Added an "add another schedule" button, enabling the users to have multiple schedules for one medicine.
  • Added an "add" button in the calendar page, enabling the users to add a medicine more intuitively.

Take-aways

It was very important to have design iterations and discuss with the team. After proposing that we should make the process of medication more flexible, we did not streamline this process until creating several alternatives and having critiques together.

Getting access to the target group is the key to understand the problem and find a solution. Asking the caregivers and patients to describe their day and seeing the artifacts they use to track the medication and symptoms helped us understand the context of the users.

It was very helpful to review all the existing products and the users' needs, which helped us find opportunities to make the apps stand out even though there are numerous medication reminder apps.
