2ndBrain

A Smart System Helping Teens with Chronic Illnesses Stay on Medication Schedule

Product Design | Interaction Design | UX Research

Introduction

31% of adolescents have at least one chronic illness. How can we improve the quality of their life with technology?

In this project with the theme of "Healthcare+Design", we worked closely with our client, an Endocrinologist and Professor, the patients and other stakeholders, and designed a mobile app integrated with wearable devices to help teenagers with chronic illness manage their medication and remind them to stay on medication routine.

My Role

I worked in a team of 5 as Product Designer and UX Researcher

  • Led the user interviews, the focus groups, and usability test
  • Involved in the design of all features and led the design of scheduling process
  • Crafted the visual and overall interaction design
Tool
  • Sketch

Problem

I know that chronic illness is going to be my lifestyle and medication adherence is a huge part of it, but I still want to experience high school at its fullest.

--Joy, a high school student with chronic illness

How can we help teenagers with chronic illness like Joy manage their complex medication needs and improve medication adherence?

Approach

We adopted the user-centered design and tried to get users and stakeholders involved in the whole process.

Solution Overview

Easy medication and symptom tracking

Smart analysis of med adherence and feeling

Simplified medication schedule management

Close collaboration with loved ones

Connection with wearable devices

Research

User Research Methods

To understand the daily life and challenges of teens with chronic illness, we did:

  • 2 interviews with teenagers with chronic illness, 6 interviews with stakeholders including experts and patients' parents, 2 patient focus groups, and 1 caregiver focus group
  • A questionnaire survey with 41 respondents from target group
Key Findings
  • Medication adherence is an important part of daily life among teens with chronic illness.
  • The current apps cannot meet their needs of managing complex medication schedule.
  • Parents are involved in the teen’s medication adherence actively.
    • They don't want people around them to be overly aware of their chronic conditions.
    • They need to track their symptoms to monitor the effect of the medication and report to the doctor.
    • Mobile App is used commmonly by the target users whose age is 12-18.
Persona

Based on the findings above, we built personas to help us understand the users' lives and needs.

Competitive Analysis

We did comparative analysis of about 20 products that support patients with chronic illness. We revisited those competitors after user research and identified some design opportunities.

Target Audience Device Scheduling Process Med friends
Some apps target young people and have a bright color style.
However, they don't have more features that fit the personality of young people.		 Most of the apps cannot be integrated with wearable devices. Most of the current apps only allow users to add one medicine that has a fixed schedule.
However, sometimes users may need to take several medicines at the same time, or they have different schedules for one medicine.		 Some apps allow users to invite caregivers.
However, they cannot have more context-aware settings about when to have the caregivers remind them.

Ideation

Exploration

We brainstormed different ideas and analyzed the pros and cons based on the feedback from users and their caregivers, the client, and some experts.

Digital Sticky Notes Adhesive Patch Smart Water Bottle
Pros The sticky notes can be attached to different artifacts in life and reminds the users. The patch can send alert without attracting the notice of others. Many users also need to drink a lot of water and track their water intake. They can be reminded every time when they drink water. Some teenagers think this water bottle is very cool.
Cons The reminder is too obvious and has many pieces to manage. It is not very practical. The technology has not been adopted widely. Boys might not want to carry a water bottle. The reminder might be obvious.
Decision

After the first round of ideation, we focused on coming up with more practical ideas and decided to design a mobile app integrated with wearable devices. We decided the following key features to make this app more user-friendly than existing products and fit the personality and needs of teenagers.

Design

Goal: How can we help users keep track of their medication and symptom?
Research Insight
  • Taking medicine on time is the biggest challenge and goal for patients
  • Users are setting alarms on phone or using calendars to help remind them
  • They want to understand whether a symptom is related to a medication
  • Caregivers create a sheet to record the medication and symptom
Solution

Easy medication and symptom tracking

  • Get a quick overview of how they are doing today and what needs to be done next
  • Mark a medication as done with one tap
  • Record the feeling and symptoms with emoji and stickers that fit teenagers’ personality and reduce the effort of manual input
Goal: How can we reduce users’ pains of complex medication scheduling?
Research Insight
  • Users may have several medicines on the same schedule, but they need to do the same setting multiple times with current apps
  • Users sometimes have different dosages and schedules for the same medicine
  • Users use nickname or photos to help them remember and recognize the medicines
Solution

Simplified medication schedule management

  • Organize the medication by medicine name and schedule
  • Add a photo and nickname for the medicine
  • Allow the flexibility of setting different dosage for different schedules
  • Select an existing schedule directly in stead of adjusting multiple settings
  • Allow the flexibility of adding multiple schedules for one medicine
Goal: How can we get the loved ones involved in patients' medication adherence?
Research Insight
  • Parents text the kid to remind and ask the kids to send them a picture of medicine to make sure the medication is taken
  • Teenagers ask for their close friends’ help to remind them at school or when hanging out
Solution

Close collaboration with loved ones

  • Invite a close friend or family member as an ally
  • Set the role of ally, when and how to notify the ally
Goal: How can we remind the users in an unobtrusive way?
Research Insight
  • They don't want people around them to be overly aware of their chronic conditions.
  • They don’t want to explain their situation again and again.
Solution

Connection with wearable devices

  • Connect with smart patch and smartwatch
  • Set the vibration strength

Iteration

We sketched on the whiteboard and created wireframes with Sketch. We had 5 rounds of iteration before testing the prototypes.

We used whiteboard to sketch ideas

Exploration of design alternatives for the homepage

We tested the prototype with 2 users in person and 6 users online, and then iterated on the design based on the user feedback. The biggest change we made was to simplify the medication scheduling process.

Design iterations based on usability tests

Feedback for V1
  • They want both a nickname of pills which helps them remember the pills, and the real name which can be auto-filled and makes sense for the parents and doctors.
  • The dosage and schedule should be at the same place rather than in separate steps.
  • They may have multiple schedules for one medicine.
Design (V2)
  • Simplified the process by combining the dosage and schedule to one page to reduce the back-and-forth between medicine and schedule.
  • Allowed users to add both nickname and official name for a medicine.
  • Added an "add another schedule" button, enabling the users to have multiple schedules for one medicine.
  • Added an "add" button in the calendar page, enabling the users to add a medicine more intuitively.
Design (V3)
  • Instead of having the users make choice at the begining, integrate “add to existing schedule” to the page of "add medicine"

Take-aways

It was very important to have design iterations and discuss with the team. After proposing that we should make the process of medication more flexible, we did not streamline this process until creating several alternatives and having critiques together.

Getting access to the target group is the key to understand the problem and find a solution. Asking the caregivers and patients to describe their day and seeing the artifacts they use to track the medication and symptoms helped us understand the context of the users.

It was very helpful to review all the existing products and the users' needs, which helped us find opportunities to make the apps stand out even though there are numerous medication reminder apps.
